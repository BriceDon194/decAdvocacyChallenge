Scheduling Report for Patrick

Where Your Presence Adds the Most Cars (and Revenue)

Executive summary

Patrick, you asked a simple question: when you show up at one shop for the day, where does it pay off most? This report uses 50 days of records across your five shops (250 shop‑days total). The clearest pattern: Shops 4 and 2 jump the most when you are there. Shop 3 moves the least—so if you love visiting your brother there, that is fine emotionally, but the numbers say it is not where you “buy” the most extra cars with your time.

Because some shops have very few “boss present” days in the data, treat the exact numbers as ballpark guides, not guarantees. The charts show every single row we have, and the scenario chart translates the pattern into what could change if you shift where you spend days.

What the data shows

Each point in Figure 1 is one shop‑day: cars fixed that day, split by whether you were there. Jitter and transparency keep all 250 points visible.

Table 1 summarizes average cars with and without you, and the lift (extra cars per day when you are there, on average).

Plain English read: Shops 4 and 2 show the largest lift. Shop 3 shows a small lift. That does not mean Shop 3 is “bad”—it means your presence there does not move the needle as much as it does at 4 and 2.

How confident can you be?

We only have 50 calendar days and 250 shop‑days. Worse, you were only recorded as “present” a handful of times at some shops (see the n_present column in Table 1). With small counts, one busy week can skew averages.

What could go wrong? The future may not match the past. Competitors, staffing, seasonality, and luck all matter. The main assumption here is that past “you there” days are a fair preview of future “you there” days at each shop.

What you should do

  1. Prioritize presence at Shops 4 and 2 when the goal is to maximize extra cars from your visits.
  2. Do not read Shop 3’s small lift as “skip family.” It means that if you have a fixed number of “Patrick on the floor” days, moving some of those days from 3 to 4 or 2 is where the spreadsheet moves most.
  3. Keep watching the numbers as you get more “you there” days at 4 and 2—those averages will stabilize.

Money picture (illustration only)

Pick a dollar amount per car so the math is easy to follow. If you use $200 revenue per car (change this to your real number), then one extra car per day is about $73,000/year if it happened five days a week for 52 weeks (200 * 5 * 52). That is not a promise—just a way to turn “cars” into “money in your head.”

Future look: simple scheduling scenarios

Figure 3 shows a what‑if: if in a year you moved some of your on‑site days from Shop 3 to another shop, how many extra cars that could add, using the lifts we measured. This is not a full simulator—it is a transparent back‑of‑the‑envelope so you can see direction and scale.

Closing

Bottom line: the data says your presence matters most at Shops 4 and 2, and least at Shop 3 in terms of extra cars per visit. Use that to steer your calendar, stay humble about sample size, and update this picture as you collect more balanced weeks at each location.
